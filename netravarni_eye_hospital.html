Motiya Bind — Understanding Cataract

Types of Cataract

A cataract clouds the eye's natural lens causing blurry vision. Identifying the type helps choose the best surgical approach.

🟦 Nuclear Cataract

Most common age-related type. Affects the centre (nucleus) of the lens. Causes gradual yellowing and blurring; often progresses over years. Vision may temporarily improve then worsen ("second sight").

🟡 Cortical Cataract

Starts at the outer edge (cortex) of the lens as white, wedge-shaped spokes. Causes glare, contrast issues, and depth perception problems — especially noticeable while driving at night.

🔵 Posterior Subcapsular (PSC)

Develops at the back of the lens capsule. Progresses faster than other types. Causes reading difficulty, glare in bright light, and halos around lights — common in diabetics and steroid users.

🟠 Congenital Cataract

Present at birth or develops in early childhood due to genetics, infection during pregnancy, or metabolic disorders. Requires early surgical intervention to prevent amblyopia (lazy eye).

🟣 Traumatic Cataract

Develops after blunt or penetrating eye injury. Can appear immediately or months/years after trauma. Urgent evaluation and early surgery often needed to prevent further complications.

How It Works

Cataract Operation

Modern phacoemulsification surgery is safe, virtually painless, and takes under 20 minutes. Most patients see clearly within 24–48 hours.

Safe · Painless · Stitch-Free
No general anaesthesia. Day-care procedure.
1
Pre-operative Evaluation

Biometry (IOL power), corneal topography, blood sugar check, and physician clearance if required.

2
Pupil Dilation & Anaesthesia

Dilating drops widen the pupil. Local anaesthetic eye drops numb the eye — no injections needed for most cases.

3
Phacoemulsification

A 2–3 mm micro-incision is made. Ultrasound probe breaks the cloudy lens into fragments and gently suctions them out.

4
IOL Implantation

A foldable Intraocular Lens (monofocal, multifocal, or toric) is inserted through the same micro-incision and positioned precisely.

5
Closure & Recovery

The incision is self-sealing — no stitches. An eye shield is applied. Vision typically improves within 24–48 hours.

6
Follow-up Care

Eye drops for 4–6 weeks. Review at Day 1, Week 1, and Month 1. Final spectacle prescription after full stabilisation.
